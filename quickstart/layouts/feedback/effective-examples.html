Specific Feedback Examples

Research Paper Introduction
Effective Example

Feedback:

In paragraph 2, your literature review mentions Smith (2019) and Jones (2020), but doesn't explain how their findings relate to your research question. Consider adding 1-2 sentences that explicitly connect their work on neural networks to your hypothesis about improved accuracy in medical diagnoses.

Why this works:
  • Identifies exact location (paragraph 2)
  • Names specific authors that need better connection
  • Explains precisely what is missing (connection to research question)
  • Suggests a specific improvement (1-2 sentences making an explicit connection)
Programming Assignment
Ineffective Example

Feedback:

Your code needs more comments. It's hard to follow what you're trying to do in some places.

Why this doesn't work:
  • Doesn't identify specific functions or sections that need comments
  • Fails to explain which parts were confusing
  • Provides no examples of where or how comments would help
Improved version:

"The data processing function on lines 45-60 lacks comments explaining the complex filtering algorithm. Consider adding inline comments for each step of the process, especially the regex pattern on line 52 and the lambda function on line 58."

Actionable Feedback Examples

Data Visualization Project
Effective Example

Feedback:

The color palette you've selected for your bar chart makes it difficult to distinguish between adjacent categories. Consider using a diverging color scheme (like viridis or RdBu) instead of the current sequential blues. Also, adding thin white borders between bars would improve readability when categories have similar values.

Why this works:
  • Identifies a specific problem (indistinguishable categories)
  • Offers concrete solutions (specific color schemes)
  • Provides an additional actionable suggestion (adding borders)
  • Explains why the change would help (improved readability)
Business Case Study
Ineffective Example

Feedback:

Your analysis section is weak. You need to dig deeper into the underlying issues and provide better insights about the company's situation.

Why this doesn't work:
  • Uses subjective language ("weak") without explaining standards
  • Provides vague directives ("dig deeper", "better insights")
  • Offers no concrete steps the author could take
  • Fails to suggest specific analytical frameworks or approaches
Improved version:

"Consider strengthening your analysis section by applying Porter's Five Forces framework to assess competitive pressures, especially regarding supplier power and threat of substitutes which are currently missing. Adding a brief SWOT analysis with 2-3 points in each category would also help identify strategic options for the company."

Balanced Feedback Examples

Essay on Environmental Policy
Effective Example

Feedback:

Your essay presents a compelling argument for carbon taxation, especially in the section where you analyze the economic impacts using recent studies (pg. 3-4). The data visualization comparing different policy outcomes is particularly effective.

To strengthen your argument further, consider addressing potential counterarguments about impacts on low-income households. Your final paragraph mentions this briefly, but expanding this section with 1-2 specific policy mechanisms (like dividend rebates) would make your proposal more robust.

Why this works:
  • Opens with specific strengths (economic analysis, data visualization)
  • Acknowledges what's working well before suggesting improvements
  • Balances praise with constructive suggestions
  • Offers specific recommendations rather than vague criticism
Marketing Campaign Proposal
Ineffective Example

Feedback:

This is a good proposal overall with some interesting ideas. However, there are several major problems with your approach to social media marketing. The budget allocation seems off and your timeline is unrealistic.

Why this doesn't work:
  • Praise is generic and non-specific ("good", "interesting")
  • Criticism outweighs positive feedback without details
  • Mentions "several major problems" but only vaguely identifies them
  • No explanation of what makes the timeline "unrealistic"
Improved version:

"Your proposal's strengths include a clear target audience analysis (pg. 2) and creative campaign taglines that align well with the brand. Regarding the social media strategy, consider reallocating resources from Facebook (currently 60% of budget) to TikTok and Instagram where your target demographic spends more time. Also, the content creation timeline could be more realistic by extending the video production phase from 2 to 4 weeks, given the number of deliverables specified."

Constructive Feedback Examples

User Interface Design
Effective Example

Feedback:

The navigation design on your mobile app prototype could better support user goals. Currently, key features like "account settings" and "order history" require 3-4 clicks to access. Users might find this frustrating during regular use.

Consider implementing a persistent bottom navigation bar with 4-5 essential destinations, including a "profile" section that houses these important user functions. This approach has worked well in similar apps like [example] and aligns with material design guidelines for mobile navigation patterns.

Why this works:
  • Frames criticism in terms of user needs rather than personal preference
  • Explains the impact of the current design (user frustration)
  • Offers a specific alternative solution with rationale
  • References industry standards and successful examples
Scientific Methodology
Ineffective Example

Feedback:

Your methodology is seriously flawed. The sample size is way too small, and I don't think your statistical approach makes any sense for this kind of study. You should probably start over with a better design.

Why this doesn't work:
  • Uses harsh, judgmental language ("seriously flawed")
  • Offers criticism without explaining standards or alternatives
  • Suggests "starting over" without salvaging existing work
  • Frames feedback as absolute judgment rather than constructive input
Improved version:

"The current sample size (n=12) may limit the statistical power of your analysis. To strengthen your methodology, consider either: 1) increasing your sample to at least 30 participants based on the effect size from your pilot data, or 2) pivoting to a mixed-methods approach that supplements your quantitative data with qualitative interviews. For your statistical analysis, a repeated-measures ANOVA might be more appropriate than multiple t-tests, as it would better account for within-subject variations while reducing Type I error."

Effective Feedback Structure

Recommended Template
  1. Context Setting: Acknowledge what you're reviewing and your understanding of goals
  2. Strengths Identification: 2-3 specific elements that work well
  3. Priority Improvements: 2-3 high-impact suggestions with rationale
  4. Specific Examples: Reference exact locations/sections
  5. Forward-Looking Suggestion: End with actionable next steps
Key Principles
  • Be Specific: Point to exact elements, not generalizations
  • Stay Objective: Focus on the work, not the person
  • Prioritize: Focus on high-impact improvements
  • Explain Why: Provide rationale for suggestions
  • Balance Tone: Be constructive and supportive

Feedback Checklist

Common Pitfalls to Avoid
Vague Generalizations

"This is good" or "This needs work" without specifics

Personal Attacks

"You didn't put much effort into this" or "You don't understand the concept"

Rewriting Instead of Reviewing

Providing your own version rather than helping improve theirs

Focusing Only on Negatives

Failing to acknowledge any strengths or effective elements

Overwhelming with Too Much Feedback

Addressing every minor issue instead of focusing on priorities
